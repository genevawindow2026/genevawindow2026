About the Exhibition

New Lens

New Lens is a product of an Arísbeo Collective effort, comprising of 15 unique reinterpretations of Geneva Window's elaborately crafted panels.

The artists have combined Clarke's original concept with their own individual vision to create works that pay homage to the spirit of Geneva Window.

Though our exhibition's seminal inspiration resides elsewhere, we hope this installation appropriately conveys the message of Clarke's most infamous work.

Dates

25 June – 18 July 2026

Venue

The Collective

Arísbeo Collective

Arísbeo Collective is made up of artists and Harry Clarke enthusiasts that have come together to celebrate the window. At the core of the collective is our Producer Daniel Gillick, Curator Joshua Dyson, and Lead Researcher Rebecca Fitzgerald. Together they have been the driving force that has spear headed the collective's debut exhibition, New Lens.

The body of the collective are of course the fifteen artists that have taken onboard the material and meaning of the window to produce bodies of work that reimagine Clarke's craftsmanship in the form of a group installation. Together we are showcasing a space of collaboration, dialogue, education, yearning, joy, and Gaelic invigoration. We hope to carry this same initiative into future projects as a group.

The Others

The Others by Seamus O’Sullivan

David Ian Bickley

The Others reimagines one of the panels from Harry Clarke's Geneva Window, itself inspired by James O'Sullivan's poem of the same title. In this video work, the layered histories of literature, stained glass, and mythic imagination are reinterpreted through contemporary lens-based media. The piece becomes a meditation on how vision is shaped not only by what we look at, but by what we look through.

Slow, deliberate tracking shots move across heritage panes of glass — surfaces marked by imperfections, repairs, and the subtle distortions of age. These panes serve as both lens and subject, filtering the world beyond into shifting patterns of colour, blur, and refraction. Footage of trees and mythic landscapes appears as if summoned from another realm, their forms transformed by the textures of the glass into ghosted presences, echoes of stories passed down through artistic and cultural memory.

The work's ambient soundscape, composed from processed bells and metallic instruments, deepens this sense of spectral resonance. The familiar clarity of a bell tone is stretched and reshaped into something otherworldly, creating a sonic environment that feels suspended between the sacred and the uncanny. This shimmering audio field evokes the distant tolling of a half-remembered ritual or a call from another time, drifting across the threshold between reality and imagination.

By weaving together imagery filtered through historic glass, mythic natural scenes, and a resonant metallic sound world, The Others becomes an exploration of permeability: between past and present, material and mythic, the seen and the felt. It invites viewers into a gentle, contemplative encounter with the idea of "the others" — the figures, stories, and landscapes that linger just beyond the clear edges of perception, waiting to be glimpsed.

Juno and the Paycock

Juno And The Paycock by Seán O'Casey

Sarah K Dunn

I am interested in creating a layered surreal pen + ink illustration in response to Juno and the Paycock. I want to echo the linework and narrow composition of Harry Clarke's stained-glass panel for Juno and the Paycock — and the stark contrast of new material wealth in a threadbare tenement apartment. I plan to use rich colours to depict the fancy furniture and décor akin to Harry Clarke's rendition — potentially with some metallic elements with the phonograph. I plan to do some research into Dublin tenements around the time the play was written to get a sense of their structure.

I imagine the composition with two versions of the tenement apartment stacked — the one with new wealth upright, and one with the apartment stripped barren upside-down. Instead of Boyle being the central figure, I want Juno to be the central character in the composition, distressed from trying to hold her family together. I feel deeply for Juno, the wife/mother who must manage everything to counteract the carelessness of her husband, Boyle — the Paycock. Juno is both the primary breadwinner and keeper of the home; her plight is a tale of women's work being undervalued and overused.

Behind Juno in the background of the upright version, I picture Boyle lounging on one of the new chairs with Joxer waiting outside the window on the periphery of their wealthy version of the apartment, ready to take advantage. In the upside-down version of the house, only Boyle is collapsed on the floor, with Juno and Mary leaving for a better life on the periphery of the barren version of the apartment.

The Dreamers

The Dreamers by Lennox Robinson

Sarah Daly

In Clarke's interpretation of Lennox Robinson's three-part play, he focuses on a strong pose of the leader, Robert Emmet. In a series of events spun through rebellion, leadership, romance, violence and passion, Emmet strengthens his character in his strive for a united Ireland.

The window's controversial inclusion of literary works by James Joyce, among other depictions deemed "inappropriate," is what ultimately led the Irish government to reject the window, refusing to install it in Geneva on the basis that it did not project the desired image of a modern Irish identity.

Looking at the window in the context of modern Irish identity in 2026, we are forced to re-evaluate the meaning of Irish identity among us as a people versus that of our government. Irishness is not confined to those of us born and bred on spuds, meat and veg, but instead it shows in those who are willing to stand up to genocide, in solidarity with others, and open our minds to a reality that we are not as far removed from as we would like to believe.

My piece, entitled "The Dreamers," is not only an illustration of Lennox Robinson's literary work and an interpretation of Harry Clarke's panel — it is also a reminder of the history that repeats itself when we ignore it, the hands of the people who supported us, and the community of Irish immigrants and diaspora who gratefully accepted and continue to accept the help of others in our own time of need. The veil that we choose to see through can reveal a lot about ourselves.

A Vision…

"You will appreciate that my difficulty has nothing to do with the artistic merit of the panels; it arises from the fact that the inclusion of scenes from certain authors as representative of Irish literature and culture would give grave offence to many of our people. This would of course make it impossible for me to agree to an exhibition in which these representations occupied a prominent place."

W.T. Cosgrave, President of the Executive Council of the Irish Free State — letter to Harry Clarke, 1930

This extract derives from a letter written by W.T. Cosgrave, President of the Executive Council of the Irish Free State, to Harry Clarke in 1930, foreshadowing the official rejection of what was later to be recognised as Clarke's seminal work Geneva Window for its intended purpose of installation in the International Labour Organisation headquarters in Geneva. Completed that same year, the window was the culmination of 5 years of painstaking dedication on the part of Clarke, whose health was by then rapidly deteriorating. He would pass away in January 1931, shortly after it became clear the government had decided against presenting the work to Geneva. Depicting the work of contemporary authors of the day, Clarke's bold choice to make sexuality a core element to the piece's theme provoked animosity and alarm amongst government officials who feared the potential public backlash should such a work of art be chosen to represent Ireland on the world stage.

Geneva Window stands as a testament to Clarke's artistic exploration of the complex, layered nature of contemporary Ireland's identity. Harry Clarke's career coincided with the burgeoning Irish Free State and the national search for a visual representation that could coherently represent the Irish nation on the world stage. As a young artist whose personal and professional connections spanned widely between artistic, political and religious circles, Geneva Window illustrates an intricate amalgamation of Irish contemporary literature, mythology and Christian symbolism.

About Harry Clarke

Harry Clarke was born in Dublin on 17th March 1889, the second son and third child of Joshua and Margaret Clarke. It would be fair to say that Clarke's path to his career was hugely influenced by his father; as a successful church decorator who made stained glass design core to his business, Joshua Clarke's profession offered his son a unique exposure to the craft he would likely have not found elsewhere. Prior to leaving school in 1903 and taking a position as a draughtsman with Thomas McNamara's architectural office in Dublin, his stained-glass work for the family business allowed him to hone his talent. McNamara was impressed and urged Clarke to focus on a career in stained glass rather than architecture.

By August 1905, Clarke had been apprenticed into his father's studio and had begun taking night classes at the Metropolitan School of Art. Within a year, he managed to obtain a scholarship to attend the college full-time. He proved a popular student and met many lifelong friends through the School of Art. It was here that he also met Margaret Crilly, who would later become his wife. A highly talented artist in her own right who maintained a successful career in portrait painting alongside assisting her husband's business, she proved one of his fiercest champions. They were married on 31st October 1914 and had three children together.

Clarke's first major commission was for nine windows in the Honan Chapel, which gave full display to the range of his remarkable skill. His use of rich colours, particularly blue, and intricateness of design interweaving light cemented his reputation as an innovative stained glass artist. He would remain in demand for the rest of his life for this work. There are 150 windows credited to Clarke in 46 locations around the world; 15 counties in Ireland can claim to be the home of at least one of Clarke's windows. He also fostered a successful career as an illustrator for the publisher Harrap, receiving acclaim for his work on The Fairy Tales of Hans Christian Andersen and Tales of Mystery and Imagination by Edgar Allan Poe. His illustrations for the War Memorial Records for World War I are exceptional in that they were the only set of records that incorporated illustration.

Clarke's committed and tireless work ethic took its toll on his already delicate constitution. Both he and his brother inherited their mother's illness, tuberculosis, which had tragically claimed her life when Harry was only fourteen. Clarke's unrelenting work schedule would be interrupted only by his ever more frequent trips to sanatoriums in Switzerland to treat his rapidly deteriorating health. He passed away in Coire on 6th January 1931 in a vain attempt to return home to Ireland before his illness claimed him. He was 41 years old. As his family had not been informed of local laws requiring grave maintenance payments after 15 years, his remains were disinterred after 1946 and moved to an unmarked communal grave.

A Cultural Revival…

It is vital to consider Clarke's methods in the context of his position within the artistic movement flourishing at the time. The Irish Arts and Crafts Movement, active from the late 1890s to the late 1920s, took its formative ideals from its English counterpart founded on the theories of John Ruskin and William Morris. The celebration of individually created handmade work from locally produced materials, the dismantling of hierarchal distinctions within art forms and the support of design instruction to improve the beauty and usefulness of everyday objects sparked interest in creating a similar outfit in Ireland. Under the chairmanship of Lord Mayo, the Irish Arts and Crafts Society's mission to expand on design quality was promoted through meetings, workshop visitations and sponsored exhibitions.

Harry Clarke's inclusion in the society's fourth exhibition in 1910 was symbolic of the increasing participation of professional individuals within the movement, drawing more attention to this new, vibrant wave of artistic creativity. He was not alone in his ambitions to expand the horizons of Irish stained glass art design. The members of An Túr Gloine studio, founded by Sarah Purser in 1903, played a key role in advancing the reputation of the Irish Arts and Crafts Movement internationally; by 1920, they were receiving commissions from the United States, Singapore, India and Canada. The enterprise boasted a predominantly female collective; Wilhelmina Geddes, Catherine O'Brien, Ethel Rhind, Beatrice Elvery and Evie Hone were the studio's most prominent creatives. They shared their studio space with fellow artists Michael Healy and Hubert McGoldrick, and glaziers Tommy Kinsella and Charlie Williams.

It is also key to note that the artists connected to the movement increasingly sought inspiration from the already-flourishing Irish Literary Revival, which was presided over by W.B. Yeats, and the Gaelic League, which sought to revitalize and preserve the Irish language and culture. The eyes of Irish artists became focused on the social and political climate of their country — a focus which became evident in their artistic output. With this in mind, it is clear that Geneva Window was very much a work of its time — a piece of art that captured the multifaceted nature of Ireland's cultural history and a resounding answer to the question of its identity.

"Geneva Window"

Member states of the ILO had been invited to donate gifts "representative of the most characteristic forms of national production of art in their respective countries." The newly formed Irish Free State government saw this as an opportunity to garner international recognition and set about contemplating what form this gift ought to take. While initial plans had floated a proposal for a series of inkstands, Clarke's acclaimed reputation as a stained-glass artist convinced both Cosgrave and assistant director of the ILO Edward J Phelan to opt for the creation of a window instead.

Clarke had to first assess the space where the window would go. He arranged two visits to Geneva in 1926, one in April and one in December, to inspect the building and its interior, which was already starting to display the gifts donated from other countries. Clarke would have observed that many of the gifts were decorative art designed with the intent of blending into the building's internal fabric. This would have worked in Clarke's favour, giving him food for thought as to where in the building would be ideal for the window to reside, as well as the subject matter of the window itself. The trips to Geneva concluded with the avowed decision that the finished work would focus on "preferably something to do with an Irish writer."

Ultimately, the work of fifteen Irish writers would be represented in the finished work. The following works were illustrated in the window, selected by both Harry Clarke and W.B. Yeats:

  • The Wayfarer — Pádraig Pearse
  • The Story Brought By Brigit — Lady Augusta Gregory
  • Saint Joan — George Bernard Shaw
  • The Playboy Of The Western World — John Millington Synge
  • The Others — Seamus O'Sullivan
  • The Demi-Gods — James Stephens
  • Juno And The Paycock — Sean O'Casey
  • The Dreamers — Lennox Robinson
  • The Countess Cathleen — W.B. Yeats
  • Mr. Gilhooley — Liam O'Flaherty
  • Deirdre — George Æ Russell

  • The Magic Glasses — George Fitzmaurice
  • The Weaver's Grave — Seamus O'Kelly

The design of the window consisted of eight panels contained in a 72 inch by 40 inch frame; each panel held a scene from two literary works, with the exception of Saint Joan, which occupied one entire panel on its own. The window exhibits Clarke's talent for colour at its most imaginative, with retrospective critiques commenting on its luminous, vibrant quality. The choice of literary scenes was pointed; the writers of these works represented an increasingly vocal faction against censorship in the Irish arts scene, which was perceived as a result of Roman Catholic conservatism. Clarke himself had written passionately on the subject for both The Irish Times and The Irish Statesman, declaring such censorship to be counterintuitive to the artistic purpose. The fluid depictions of masculinity and bold depictions of sexual love and desire were intentionally blended with religious imagery — an indication that Clarke's vision of the new state was one of acknowledgement of all elements of the Irish cultural tapestry. Unfortunately, the prospect of this all-encompassing artistic vision being displayed on the world stage was almost immediately stopped in its tracks.

The window was presented to W.T. Cosgrave, President of the Executive Council of the Irish Free State, in September 1930. While initially the window was well-received by both the government and the press as a work of outstanding artistic merit, the dissent relating to Clarke's chosen subject was immediate and forceful. Clarke was under pressure to change two particular panels and was urged not to bow to the demand by Yeats and several other friends. A meeting was proposed between Clarke and a government representative at his studio, during which it was hoped that some form of compromise might be reached on both sides. This meeting never took place, and Clarke's repeated requests for a decision regarding his window went unanswered until after his death. The government ultimately decided against using the window but still elected to purchase it. It was consigned to storage until Margaret Clarke bought back the window for the price of £450. It would go on to be exhibited at the Fine Arts Society in London in 1988, before it was acquired by the Wolfsonian Foundation of Decorative Propaganda and Art. It resides in the foundation's home gallery in Miami to this day.

Censorship

When we contemplate the government's hesitancy and eventual refusal to commit to the choice of Clarke's work as a representation of the Irish state on the world stage, it is important to keep in mind the legislation that had been passed only a year prior to Clarke's presentation of Geneva Window to government officials. The Censorship of Publications Act, passed in 1929, created a board that would oversee the banning of books and periodicals deemed unfit for public consumption. The criteria for this ban largely rested on the Catholic Church's definition of "blasphemous" material and a puritanical aversion on the part of the Church and its lay organisations to "contaminants" assuming a presence in Irish culture. In fact, the groundwork for the passing of this law, which would go on to severely restrict freedom across intellectual and artistic paradigms in the state, had been the work of several years prior to Geneva Window's unveiling.

The basis of the Censorship Act can be traced to 1926, when, caving to pressure from a Catholic pressure group known as The Irish Vigilance Association and following a meeting with Catholic bishops, Minister for Justice Kevin O'Higgins established The Committee for Enquiry on Evil Literature. They submitted their first report the very same year of their formation; this report would provide the framework for the establishment of the Censorship Act. The objectives of the Committee were driven by the fervent fundamentalism of members who hailed from various Catholic lay organisations; as well as The Irish Vigilance Association, members of the CTSI (Catholic Truth Society of Ireland) and The Knights of Saint Columbanus also played a role within this new formation. It provided these groups, already well established in their proclivity towards censorship, with a mandate to escalate their "crusade" against what they deemed "inappropriate" in the Irish cultural sphere. Their influence on the public was largely based on the success of these groups, in particular the CTSI, in establishing parish-based cadres that sought to stem the circulation of publications that did not meet their moral criteria.

The Catholic Church and their lay groups were also well versed in the utilisation of moral pressure and threats against individuals and businesses that refused to bow to their demands. W.T. Cosgrave encountered this first hand in October 1929, when conflict erupted over the rejection of a request from a member of the Order of Saint Gregory that the book Ropers Row be censored. The complainant, one F. O'Reilly, wrote to Cosgrave to assert that he "did not intend to let the matter rest" and would "publish their correspondence with comments" if the president did not comply with his request. Though the president's office cryptically noted the deployment of threats by O'Reilly "as an endeavour to get what he wants," and that to concede to him "would invite further trouble," Cosgrave ultimately chose to give way in spite of the displeasure of his office at doing so.

In light of this contextual background, the letter from Cosgrave to Clarke voicing his misgivings over the content of the window illuminates how the government found itself torn between an eagerness to present a successful Irish artist on the world stage and anxiety over the need to toe the line with a Catholic Church that exerted a dominating influence over the Irish state's public moral conduct. Government officials had actively promoted Clarke as the preferred candidate for the ILO commission from the outset; a letter from Gordon Campbell to Clarke in 1925 informed him that "the Irish delegation…in Geneva was strongly in favour of the proposed gift of stained glass." Clarke's appeal lay in his innovative style, an aesthetic that could promote the concept of Irish modernity. This desire to promote a culture imbued with the modern abroad ultimately clashed with the obligations of acting within the domestic conservative confines of what was deemed appropriate when the window's subject focus was revealed.

The suggestion of changing the panels was clearly an attempt at compromise; following Clarke's refusal to do so, the situation regressed into an aversion, on the part of the government, to state what their intention was with regard to Geneva Window, until after Clarke's death. Any wish to avoid infringing on Clarke's artistic freedom was ultimately overruled by the established government line of not provoking an outcry from influential conservative figures who dictated what constituted Irish moral sentiment. The Geneva Window and the conflict that ensued in the aftermath of its creation offers a stark perspective of an Irish artistic sphere struggling against the weight of a society bent on constraining individual creative freedom for the sake of a repressive moral code.
